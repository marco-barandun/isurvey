Plot design EDGG standardised methodology

Dengler et al. 2016 (Bull. EDGG 32: 13–30) + second amendment (Dengler, Biurrun & Dembicz 2021, Palaearctic Grasslands 49: 22–26). A 100 m² square (10×10 m), with a full nested subplot series (0.0001–10 m²) replicated in two opposite corners (NW, SE).

Setup instructions (tape-measure procedure)

100 m² plot: mark a starting point, fix a tape and mark 14.14 m along it (the NE–SW diagonal) — this corner isn't used for subplots. Pull both ends out to 10 m to square off the NW and SE corners (the ones that do get the nested series). Use fibreglass tape, not metal — it distorts the square. Mark corners with pegs; buried magnets are recommended for permanent re-location.

Nested series (each of NW & SE): from the corner, lay out 3.16 m sides for the 10 m² plot. Within it, 1 m² and 0.1 m² (32 cm) are laid out with a folding rule bent at 90°. The three smallest sizes — 0.01 m² (10 cm), 0.001 m² (3.2 cm), 0.0001 m² (1 cm) — are usually not physically marked (to avoid disturbing the vegetation); instead just check with a folding rule whether each plant is within that distance of the corner.

1000 m² plot: concentric with the 100 m² plot (same centre, same orientation) — its corners lie further out along the same diagonal lines. From the original starting point, extend the diagonal to 29.43 m for the far corner (opposite the 14.14 m corner); mark the outline with coloured string. See Dengler, Biurrun & Dembicz (2021) Fig. 2 for the full 15-step sequence.

100 m² cover check

Amendment 2021, Addition 2: not exhaustive — only estimate cover here for (a) species present in the wider 100 m² but absent from both 10 m² corners, and (b) species whose true 100 m² cover clearly differs from the average of the two 10 m² estimates. Use a filled-square estimation aid: at 100 m², 1% cover ≈ a 1.00×1.00 m square, 5% ≈ 2.24×2.24 m, 0.1% ≈ 0.32×0.32 m (see Table 1 of the 2021 amendment for the full reference).

Species database

Bundled taxon lists used for offline search. Add more packs as JSON files in species/ — see species/README.md.

Defaults

Location capture takes 10 GPS readings and averages them, keeping only fixes at or below this accuracy. Modern phone GPS (e.g. iPhone 14 Pro and later, with dual-frequency GNSS) typically reaches 3–8 m in open sky — 10 m is a good default. Loosen it to 15–20 m under forest canopy or near buildings if capture struggles to complete.

Voice logging

In a relevé's species list or a transect, "Start voice logging" listens continuously so you can walk hands-free and call out species as you spot them. Confident matches are added right away; unsure matches are still added but flagged for you to check afterwards. If nothing usable is heard, it asks you to repeat by voice. Needs a connection (Google/Apple speech recognition), same as single-field dictation.

While voice logging a transect, you get a gentle (non-interrupting) nudge every time this many taxa have been logged, as a natural point to open Review & approve. You can always review sooner, or wait until the end — this is just a reminder.

Set this to your accent. Latin names are recognised far better when the speech engine knows which language you're pronouncing them in — this is the single biggest thing you can do for accuracy if you speak with an Italian, German or French accent. Applies to both standard dictation and the AI option below.

When matching a spoken name, favours species actually recorded near your location — anywhere in the world, weighted by distance across a wide area (with a bundled general-commonness fallback for offline). A likely plant wins over a rare look-alike. Relaxed: it only nudges the ranking, never hides anything.

AI-enhanced voice matching experimental

Instead of trusting a generic dictation engine's guess at what you said, this records your voice, runs it through an on-device speech model, and directly scores the audio against every plausible species name in the checklist — picking whichever name the recording actually sounds most like, rather than matching text after the fact. Meant to noticeably outperform standard dictation on Latin names it was never trained on.

Tradeoffs: the model downloads once over a connection the first time you turn this on, then is cached by the browser and works offline after that. Recognition runs on your device's CPU, so each species takes a few seconds and uses more battery than standard dictation — including in "Start voice logging" while walking a relevé or transect, which also switches to this engine when enabled. Two names said back-to-back with too short a pause between them may be missed (no equivalent yet of the text-based multi-species splitting standard dictation uses).

The larger model is markedly better at the unusual Latin names this app depends on. Changing this takes effect on the next app reload.
